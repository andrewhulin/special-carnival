# Feb 16 - Practicing therapist, uses for clients & supervision

Interview Date: February 16, 2026
Interviewer: Clare Flanagan
Key Themes: Clinical use case, Comparison to therapy, Relational bonding with AI, Wants expertise, Warmth/gentleness
Paid v. Organic: Paid
Primary Persona: Coach
User Description: Practicing therapist who uses Ash personally and recommends it to clients as between-session support. Uses Ash to get clinical supervision on challenging cases and to explore her own relationship issues. Focuses on relational therapy and somatics.

4:15pm, 2.16.26

## Intro: background + how she started using Ash

- Is a practicing therapist!!
- Saw it on Facebook, maybe - not sure how or where she found it.
- "It's [made] by therapists, from what I understand… I do refer my clients to it at times."
    - I was curious: in what situations does she tend to recommend it?
        - "I have a lot of clients that kind of struggle during the week… it's kind of an added support in a way. They can't necessarily call me all the time."
        - "I'm finding - and I know you have to be careful with AI - but I'm really finding that instead of journaling and nothing coming back, this is in a way of journaling and having a perspective."
        - "But I also feel like it's a coach. It helps me to think about things maybe differently than I would have thought about them before."
- Then, she shared something really fascinating - she uses it to get ideas and advice on clinical scenarios where she feels stuck!
    - "Some of it I use for my own relationship stuff, or if I have a client where I want to check to see [how] I'm doing… [I wonder], does it have a better suggestion? Am I on track here?"
        - Example: she described a struggling couple - specifically, a husband wants to build more intimacy and openness with his wife. They're working on trying to build trust so he can ask for physical intimacy directly, and then she can say whether or not she's comfortable with it. If he gets a no, then he feels rejected and lonely - which, in the therapist's view, "touches on his aloneness" that he suffered growing up.
        - Complicating factor: he had a 90-minute conversation with someone on a plane and ended up feeling really drawn to them. She told him "you're kind of having a fantasy about this, about what you're going to get from someone. They're not going to be everything you're hoping that they will be… so we need to work on your aloneness, which is the part that's being activated right now."
        - At this, the client got a little bit upset, finding that her use of the word "fantasy" invalidated his feelings.
        - After asking Ash for advice on what to say to him, it suggested this: "When I said it could have been a fantasy, I can see why it could have been dismissive of your longing. That wasn't my intention. My question is whether changing partners would help the loneliness."
- What led her to turn to Ash in that moment, I wondered?
    - "His reaction. Because [there was] a little bit of a rupture, I wanted to know what it was about that statement that felt so invalidating."
        - This is a really fascinating use case! If Ash can help users gain new perspectives on their own issues, could it also help therapists see tricky client situations from a different angle - especially moments during moments of rupture or misunderstanding?
- I shared with her that we are thinking about making a version of Ash that works alongside therapy. How would she envision that working, if she were to use it in her own practice?
    - Her first question: would sessions be recorded? I said that it could be a possibility; it would depend on what she (and other therapists) find useful.
    - She then said: "It could hold the theme of the session… if they have some feelings or thoughts about what we worked on during the week, they could possibly input some of that into Ash."
    - "I might be a little concerned about… in a sense, are they offloading it elsewhere rather than bringing it into the session with me by doing that during the week?"
        - An important consideration to keep in mind! If we were to build a version of Ash that could be used concurrently with therapy, we might want to establish some role separation - i.e. clarifying what kinds of things users should turn to Ash for vs. what they should "save" for in-person sessions.
    - Clients might be able to use Ash between sessions as an "adjunct" - a way to "continue the work."
- I then asked: if you had access to a user's conversations with Ash, how might you leverage them?
    - "I would deepen the conversations" the user had with Ash.
    - "I'm a very relational therapist, and [therapy] is about our relationship… undoing people's aloneness, being with them, helping people drop more into their feelings."
    - "I do find that with AI… it sometimes encourages you to slow down and engage with your feelings."
        - Overall, she seemed to be expressing that there is a relational component to therapy that can't be replicated in conversations with Ash - although Ash conversations could be a useful jumping-off point to deepen that relationship.
- Would you have any concerns about using Ash as a supplement to therapy, or as part of your own practice?
    - "I'm pretty familiar with how to use therapy… so using Ash just helps me to think about things that I can bring into the therapy in a different way and deepen what I'm already working on. It can make me think about things that I might not be thinking about."
    - "Now, I will say that what it also does is that it forces me not to think as much. Because it's giving me the answers in certain ways. And I don't know how it could be configured where, instead of giving the answer - which it will do, like, you could say this like this - it would be more helpful to my learning if it said, let's have you frame that first."
    - "And I don't want it to kiss my ass, okay? So it might say all right, that's close. Could you think about that and try it again? So it would make me do the work rather than doing it for me."
        - Very important insight here that mirrors a concern I have about foundation models vs. slg - they seem to be telling the user what to think/doing more of the work for them, while slg (in my opinion) would ask more probing questions that didn't always have clear answers.

## Feedback on her personal experience using Ash

- "In a way… I know it's weird, and I don't know how people feel about it… I'm getting some connection here. I know I have to be careful, because people fall in love with AI… It can be so kind and sweet, and that's really nice, and it does feel good."
    - This was the second time in the interview that she expressed some hesitation and caution about the kind of "bond" that can be built with AI - it seems like it's something she keeps in mind, but it hasn't stopped her from using the product.
- "I also use Claude, and I find Claude to be very direct. It will call you out! And I can't remember if Ash does that or not."
- "But it does have really nice insights, and the insights are right on… it does have that component of making me think about something."
    - I've gotten a boatload of positive feedback from users on the quality of Ash's weekly insights!!

## Final piece of feedback - given after she asked about our staff therapists + the modalities they specialize in.

- "I think you need a trauma therapist on your staff."
    - She elaborated: "Most of the people who are coming [to therapy] are dealing with relational trauma. That's what happens to most of us. You want to address somatics… you want to try to help people drop into their feelings, and what's going to come up is possibly anxiety or defense… so I think some of that is [helped] by just really seeing them and what they're expressing, and suggesting maybe they reach out to someone else to connect with."
    - "It's great to give prompts for meditation and breathing exercises [or temperature based exercises]. Those are all ways of regulating and that's helpful. But if possible, you also want to get to… and I don't know how that would look… feeling accompanied, not alone, helping them check in on what's going on inside, making space for that, holding that… I don't know how you would do that… without another person in the room and really being there."
        - Perhaps another argument for Ash + Therapy… because Ash can provide a lot of perspective and in-the-moment support, but maybe it can't quite replicate the relational security that helps a lot of people feel safe sitting with and working through their feelings?
