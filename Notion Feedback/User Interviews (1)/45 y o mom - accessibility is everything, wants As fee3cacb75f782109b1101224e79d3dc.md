# 45 y/o mom - accessibility is everything, wants Ash to feel more human

Interview Date: February 26, 2026
Interviewer: Andrew Frawley
Key Themes: Been to therapy
Paid v. Organic: Organic
Primary Persona: N/A
User Description: June is a 45-year-old married mother of 12-year-old twins in therapy for anxiety. She found Ash through a research study comparing AI therapy apps. She loves the accessibility but finds Ash too verbose and "Google-like" compared to a competitor (Aurie) that feels more like talking to a real person.

### Intro & Background

June is married (18 years), has boy-girl twins age 12, works part-time, and volunteers heavily with PTA. She deals with anxiety and is currently in therapy. She found Ash not through ads or referrals but through a **research study** that recruited therapy users to compare multiple AI mental health apps. She was given Ash plus two others (including Aurie) and asked to compare them. She had no prior awareness that AI therapy companions existed: *"I didn't even know about this. I didn't even know that there was an AI companion to this."*

She's been using both Ash and Aurie simultaneously and continues to do so, giving her a rare direct-comparison perspective.

---

### Background with Mental Health / Self-Development

June is actively in therapy and manages anxiety. She didn't go deep into her therapy history but described a common frustration with the traditional model — sessions are infrequent, scheduled weeks out, and feel like they need to be reserved for "big moments": *"When you're in therapy, you're packing away your big moments. So when you talk to your therapist, you're making those forty minutes to an hour count."*

No mention of self-development books, coaching, or prior AI mental health use before the research study.

---

### Marketing & Expectations

**Discovery:** Research study recruiting people already in therapy to compare AI mental health apps.

**Expectations going in:** She had no expectations — she didn't know the category existed. The study gave her a framework for comparison she wouldn't have had otherwise.

**What Ash does right:**

- **Accessibility is the core value.** This was her dominant theme, repeated multiple times with genuine enthusiasm: *"Even if it's something small… it's right there. It's accessible, and that's what I really liked about it."* She was emphatic that this alone is transformative: *"My mind is just blown that this is accessible to us."*
- **Good advice and prompts.** She acknowledged that Ash's actual content and guidance is strong: *"As far as both of them, they give me great advice. Don't get me wrong."*
- **Prevents escalation.** She articulated the preventive value clearly — small moments addressed in real-time don't become big moments: *"What helps is with that something small, it doesn't get into something bigger."*
- **No judgment.** *"Even if I'm like, I'm so angry… they're not judging you."*

**What Ash gets wrong — and where a competitor is winning:**

This is the most direct competitive feedback in any interview. June is actively using Aurie alongside Ash and finds Aurie more engaging:

- **Ash feels like Googling.** *"It feels like I'm literally googling. The answers are more canned… it gives me longer responses, but almost like it copy-pasted from Google."*
- **Too verbose, too many questions at once.** *"It gave me like a long answer… and then it gave me two, three [prompts]. And I was like, well, which one should I answer first?"*
- **Missing active listening cues.** Her most specific and actionable feedback: Ash lacks the small conversational signals — "mhmm," "that makes sense," "I get it" — that make a conversation feel like someone is present and listening rather than waiting to deliver a response: *"It would make you feel like, oh, damn. Like, yeah… I think the little tweaks like that would make a difference."*
- **Aurie feels like talking to a person.** *"With Aurie… it feels more like I'm actually speaking to a therapist. Smaller prompts and it lets me actually speak more."* She stays in Aurie sessions longer as a result: *"I last longer with Aurie because it does feel like I'm actually speaking to someone."*

---

### Product Positioning

**Companion vs. clinical:** June sees Ash as a **companion to therapy**, not a replacement — and she thinks this is exactly how it should be positioned and distributed: *"If you guys made it as a companion to therapy, I think that would help a lot of people… I belong to Kaiser Permanente, and I think if they offered this as a companion, I think more people would be willing to try it."*

**Personality / intervention preferences:** She wants shorter responses, one question at a time, and active listening signals (verbal nods, empathic micro-responses). She's essentially asking for Ash to feel less like an expert delivering a report and more like a friend leaning in.

**Social/community feature:** Clear no for herself. She sees the good intention but worries about safety and bad actors: *"How can you guarantee that someone is not going to get advice that's gonna send them over the edge?"* She suggested making it an opt-in toggle rather than a default.

---

### Closing Insights & Strategic Takeaways

**For marketing & positioning:**
June's "companion to therapy" framing is the cleanest B2B positioning insight in these interviews. She didn't just say Ash complements therapy — she named a specific payer (Kaiser Permanente) and described a distribution model: health plans offering Ash as an adjunct benefit. This validates a potential channel strategy: **partner with insurers/providers to position Ash as a between-session companion**, not a standalone therapy replacement.

Her "small moments prevent big moments" framing is also powerful for consumer messaging — it's intuitive, relatable to any parent or anxious person, and doesn't require the user to identify as "needing therapy."

**On the "daily tool" question:**
June's usage is naturally daily-adjacent because parenting generates constant micro-stressors. She doesn't need Ash to manufacture reasons to check in — she needs it to be there in the moments between pickup and drop-off, between arguments and bedtime. Her framing: *"It doesn't have to be a big moment."*

**Key product signals:**

- **Ash is losing engagement to a competitor on conversational feel.** This is the most urgent product signal across all interviews. June is a dual user and explicitly prefers Aurie's shorter, more human-feeling exchanges. Ash's verbosity and multi-question responses are pushing an active user toward a rival.
- **Active listening cues ("mhmm," "that makes sense")** are a low-lift, high-impact UX improvement that could meaningfully change how human Ash feels — especially in voice mode.
- **Response length and question density** need to be reduced. Multiple interviewees have flagged this (Christopher, June), but June provides the competitive proof point: shorter responses = longer sessions = higher retention.

**Acquisition insight:**
First user acquired through a third-party research study. This is an unusual channel but suggests that **clinical research partnerships** could serve double duty as both evidence-building and user acquisition pipelines. Users recruited this way are pre-qualified (already in therapy) and comparative (they've tried alternatives), making them high-signal for product feedback.
