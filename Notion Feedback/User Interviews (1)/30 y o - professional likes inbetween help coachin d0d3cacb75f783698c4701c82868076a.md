User Description: Amanda is a 30-year-old senior PM in Toronto working at a payer-provider insurance company with in-house mental health services. She found Ash through a colleague, uses it as a real-time decision-processing companion for moments too small or urgent for therapy — and increasingly as an informal career coach.

### Intro & Background

Amanda works in product management at a Canadian insurance company that operates as both payer and provider, giving her a professional lens on mental health access and coverage. She has excellent therapy benefits ($10,000/year for mental health counseling) and actively sees a psychotherapist. A colleague who also goes to therapy recommended Ash over dinner, framing it as a free tool that fills gaps between sessions. Amanda downloaded it and took to it quickly, though she prefers typing over the voice feature.

Her initial motivation was very specific: she's casually job-searching, and the interview process has been stressful. Ash became a place to process that stress in real time.

---

### Product Expectations & Feedback

**Core use case — micro-decisions and real-time processing:**
Amanda's usage pattern centers on moments where she's caught between two options and starting to spiral. She can't call her therapist at 10PM or on a Sunday evening, and by the time she could get an appointment, the moment has passed:

> *"I find I use Ash in those moments where I'm like, oh, I have two options and help me pick the better one of the two so that I don't go down a spiral."*
> 

> *"If it's Sunday 6PM and you're feeling what I call the Sunday scaries, you can't necessarily call her up… and sometimes she can't even get me in until a couple of days. And then at that point… I'm like, what did I need the appointment for?"*
> 

This positions Ash not as a therapy replacement but as a **time-sensitive companion** for moments that are real but perishable.

**What Ash does right:**

- **Zero barrier to entry.** Amanda contrasted Ash's instant onboarding against the friction of finding a therapist — free intro calls, mismatches, trial and error: *"I find Ash, like, you can download it and get going right away."* She sees this as valuable across the entire mental health journey spectrum: people who've never tried therapy, people actively in therapy, and people who've run out of coverage.
- **Contextual memory.** She values that Ash retains context about her life and can help her weigh decisions with that backdrop: *"Helping me process which one makes more sense given all the context that it knows about me."*
- **Blurs the therapy/coaching line productively.** Amanda has organically used Ash as a career coach — processing job search stress, salary comparisons, stay-or-go decisions. She noted her therapist also lets conversations bleed into career territory, but she's aware not all therapists do: *"A friend of mine sees a therapist, and she kind of cuts the conversation short when it gets into career stuff because it's like, now you're just complaining."* Ash doesn't have that boundary, and Amanda sees that as a feature.

**What's missing / could improve:**
Amanda didn't surface major product complaints in this interview. Her feedback was more structural/positional than feature-driven.

---

### Closing Insights & Strategic Takeaways

**On the coaching vs. therapy identity:**
This interview surfaced a rich tension around positioning. Amanda sees therapy and coaching as deeply intertwined — *"therapy is a form of coaching… coaching you how to approach situations, how to rewire your brain"* — but recognizes that "coach" carries baggage: *"Buy my course and then learn how to sell courses to other people… coaching gets a bad rap from that."*

She also noted that people don't want to pay out of pocket for coaching the way they will for therapy, which is a recognized, covered profession in Canada. Her insight: *"People gravitate towards things that they're familiar with… maybe I need therapy, but oh, I need a career coach or a life coach — what the hell is a life coach?"*

**For marketing & positioning:**
Amanda represents a **well-resourced user who still has gaps** — she has $10K in annual therapy coverage and a great therapist, yet Ash fills a role neither can. This challenges the assumption that Ash's primary audience is underserved populations. Her use case suggests a positioning angle around **"even if you have great therapy, you still need something for the moments in between."**

Her professional vantage point in health insurance also makes her a credible voice on Ash's value across the coverage spectrum — from never-tried-therapy to coverage-exhausted.

**On the "daily tool" question:**
Amanda's usage is already quasi-daily and organic, driven by the natural cadence of career stress and micro-decisions. She didn't need to be sold on frequency — she needed Ash to be available when her therapist wasn't. The implication: for users like Amanda, Ash doesn't need to manufacture daily engagement; it just needs to be reliably present for the unpredictable moments that already happen every day.
