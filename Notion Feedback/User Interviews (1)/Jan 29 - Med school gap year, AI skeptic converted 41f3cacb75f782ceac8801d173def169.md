# Jan 29 - Med school gap year, AI skeptic converted

Interview Date: January 29, 2026
Interviewer: Clare Flanagan
Key Themes: AI skeptic converted, Comparison to therapy, Prefers directness, UX copy feedback, Wants expertise
Paid v. Organic: Paid
Primary Persona: Coach
User Description: Female in early 20s, recent undergrad graduate taking gap year while applying to med school. Found Ash after learning about AI for text message help, searched specifically for "AI Therapy" because she wanted guidance without troubling others but was skeptical of AI being a "yes man."

## Intro - background info + why she started using Ash

- Female in her early 20s - just graduated from undergrad, taking gap year while applying to med school.
- Discovered Ash after learning that a friend of hers had been using AI to help her re-phrase a difficult text message in a way that "wasn't toxic" - this use case seemed to intrigue/appeal to her.
- "I sometimes feel certain ways - like I want to get reassurance or guidance on minor problems - and I feel like I'm troubling others. So I looked up 'AI Therapy' on the App Store and found Ash."
    - At this, I asked a follow-up question: why search for "AI Therapy" specifically, instead of something more like a companion or an assistant?
        - "I did try using ChatGPT but I think it's very much an enabler. It's been well established… I told someone I was using Chat and they were like, don't do that. It's a yes man." (!!!)
        - "Sometimes you're just in the wrong. And certain platforms will support the way you phrase stuff or act, even if it's not right… that's why I was hesitant to use a platform that was AI."
            - Very interesting to note this level of skepticism and caution around the sycophancy/disingenuousness of AI, even in a young, tech-savvy person!
    - After she shared this, I wanted to know: do you perceive Ash as a "yes man?"
        - No, she said - "Ash is less of a "yes man." It does support me well, but it doesn't always give me answers… my friends have said that when they go to therapy in-person, they don't give answers either. Ash is similar in that it's not always about finding a solution, it's more about regulating your feelings."
            - Note: this is the second user I talked to today who has not been to in-person therapy, but assumes that the Ash experience must be similar (and actually appreciates that).
        - Acknowledged that "I want answers, which I know are hard to get. But I also just want support."
        - Appreciated the quality of Ash's insight: "Sometimes it clocks me really well on things that I was saying that I wasn't noticing."
        - Also (significantly)! Ash has inspired her to seek out in-person therapy.
            - "It gave me a lot of awareness - it showed me that I have issues that I should maybe talk to a licensed professional about."
    - Like other users I've spoken to, she emphasized Ash's role as a tool for self-understanding, rather than a separate or "real" entity that she has a distinct relationship with.
        - "It's nice to have a place where I can vent and get all my thoughts out, but it also asks thought-provoking questions questions that let you have a conversation with yourself."

## Persona-specific feedback

- When she opened the menu, the "Bestie" and "Coach" options caught her eye. She decided to give Coach a try - was attracted to the "straight to solving your problems" language in the descriptive copy.
- What led to her choosing Coach, aside from the appeal of problem-solving?
    - "I saw that there was a feature that was more enthusiastic and warm, but I didn't want it to be enabling… I want to test the enthusiastic feature, but if it enables me, I don't want to forget that I'm on that feature that does that."
        - Again, I'm noticing a desire to remain connected with reality and maintain an awareness that AI can be inaccurate.
        - Interesting to note, too, that people might opt against "Bestie" or other similarly-positioned personas because they don't want to be enabled or simply told "yes."
- What differences did you notice between Coach and the original Ash experience?
    - She finds that Original Ash has a pretty distinct reflective capacity. Specifically, it will pick out and repeat back certain thoughts that she shares, but with a slightly different spin or interpretation. This helps her both become more aware of her own thoughts and feelings, and potentially gain a new perspective on the situation.
        - Expressed healthy skepticism + awareness once again: "I learn from what it says, but I also take stuff with a grain of salt because it's an AI. And ultimately, this is my life."
    - In contrast, Coach gives her more actionable, straightforward ways to cope. For example, she's had problems focusing on studying, and Coach suggested planning a 60-90 minute focus block, which she found helpful.
        - At one point, it also suggested 5-4-3-2-1 grounding in a moment of anxiety, which she found effective. But she also expressed a desire to be able to use these strategies independently - "it's just a question of teaching myself how to do this in real life."
        - Overall, she liked "having the option of something more solution-based."
- I then asked how she envisioned herself using the different personas going forward. Does she envision herself switching to Coach when she specifically needs problem-solving help?
    - "I think I would possible switch to different ones, but in transparency, most of the time I know my feelings. I just need a solution. So I see myself staying with coach."
    - She did express some openness to trying other personas: "If I wanted to explore my values, I might do "the real one… and if I wasn't getting the answer I wanted, maybe I'd try Bestie. But I don't think I would switch, honestly…"
        - Overall, she seems to know what she likes - in line with the pick-and-stick persona usage pattern we've been seeing!

## Closing thoughts

- "I envision a mix of both [Ash and real-life therapy]… I want to try real therapy because there are things I'd need a licensed professional for. But I could still see myself using Ash for things like helping me write [text] messages [in difficult social situations]."
    - Supports @Andrew Frawley's observation that many young people use AI as a kind of social analysis/assistance tool.
- Also mentioned the immediacy/convenience factor, which many users have brought up.
    - "There are times where therapy is not a 24/7 solution. If I need help sending a [text] message at 11pm I can't call a therapist, but I can ask Ash, what is a healthy process here? Because it asks me helpful questions like, 'what do you see yourself getting out of this conversation? How is this important to you?' And that helps me figure out my OWN answers."
        - Nice to see that Ash isn't doing the work for this particular user - instead, it's empowering her to get clear on her priorities and find her own voice. : )
- Interestingly, she remained open to ChatGPT use… but more as an alternate perspective, and not something she'd feel comfortable exclusively relying on.
    - "If Ash doesn't provide me with solutions, I might cross-check with chat… like, 'let me see the other side, the yes man, and see how it compares."
