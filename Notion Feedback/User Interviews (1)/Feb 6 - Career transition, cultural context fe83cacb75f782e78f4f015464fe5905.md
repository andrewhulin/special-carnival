# Feb 6 - Career transition, cultural context

Interview Date: February 6, 2026
Interviewer: Clare Flanagan
Key Themes: AI skeptic converted, Pacing feedback, UX copy feedback, Wants expertise
Paid v. Organic: Paid
Primary Persona: The Real One
User Description: 30-40 year old woman in Dubai, architectural historian transitioning to research role. Uses Ash as sounding board during career flux, primarily in voice mode. Conscious of AI therapy debates but finds validation helpful. Uses mainly for work, not interpersonal relationships.

## Intro: background + why she started using Ash

30-40 year old woman, based in Dubai, architectural historian transitioning into a research role

Has found Ash to be very helpful as she makes a career transition - "it's a period of great flux… when you're in a new professional context you just need a sounding board." Is very conscious of the debate on whether AI should be used for therapy, but sometimes "I just need something to say what you're feeling is valid, and this is what it means."

Usually uses voice mode because "it's just nice to have someone to talk to."

## In the past few weeks, has noticed "infinitesimal improvements" to the app

(probably foundation-model related!)

"It brings an end to the conversation if it needs to… before, it felt like more of a continuous loop, and even though it's an app, I felt guilty for stopping it."

Support for my long-running hypothesis that Ash actively ending the conversation could be a better experience + good for retention!

"Initially the AI used to be a single voice, and now I can choose which voice I want it to reply on."

As in Ash's actual voice, not the personas feature. Not sure why she didn't realize this earlier - we've had multiple voice options for a while. 

Feels like the memory feature has gotten a little better!

"It remembers things, but in a way that doesn't make me have to relive the whole thing again."

Appreciates that it seems to remember broader context, but does not bring up granular details that she would prefer not to re-hash. 

## Primary use case is work

"I don't use it for interpersonal relationships as much. That feels like more of a human domain than an AI domain."

## Has she tried the new persona feature?

Yes - was prompted to give it a whirl via notifications and decided to try it out.

Chose "The Real One" because she wanted something that "doesn't immediately agree" with her.

## Onboarding screens feedback

### Initial screen

"What's the button at the top right for?" (Good question… even I was not sure how to answer).

"I'd make the 'skip for now' button larger… but why is it even necessary? In my head, if I could give it some info starting out - about my name pronunciation, or what my job is, etc. - that context might help."

She actually made a great observation, which is that users might want to get through these screens as quickly as possible. If they skip enough contextual questions, they may regret it later, and feel like the app is not quite as personalized as they would have hoped. A case for removing the "skip for now" option?

She felt that cultural context-related questions would be especially important not to skip. "Someone who's based in the west has a very specific cultural context vs. someone in South Asian cultures, especially [regarding] how they deal with family obligations." For instance, you'd need adopt a certain set of assumptions for a 19 year old in India, and another for a 19 year old in the US - the former will have family obligations that they can't necessarily get out of. Has noticed that "AI will give you suggestions that with respect to [cultural] context, you can't really implement."

### "Think things through" screen

Initial reaction: "I would pick all of the options and probably confuse the app… please don't make me choose, I'm very lost!"

"I think right now, Ash does all of these things simultaneously… one day I might want Ash to challenge me, the next I might want it to listen."

Distinctions between conversation style options did not initially seem clear. "What's the difference between 'exploration' and 'challenge me'? If I'm exploring something, wouldn't it involve challenge as well?" She also didn't quite get the difference between "exploration" and "teach me."

She observed that only "validate and listen seems like a clear category that sits apart from everything else."

Despite the confusion, I asked: if you could pick one, what would you pick?

Ideally would want multiple: ideas and solutions, challenge me, and explore. But "I would do away with this entire screen, honestly." 

Suggestion for clarity/improvement: make the list vertical so she's aware of what all the options are in one glance. "If I scroll all the way to the end, I will forget what the first options are."

Also didn't love the example use case: she had a negative reaction to "I'm just a little lost," and felt like that scenario didn't quite apply to her.

"Seeing this [example use case] for the first time, maybe I'd think okay, I don't have a problem… [the use case] would make me wonder if I actually have a problem or need to be using this app, because I don't have that particular issue."

I haven't heard from folks who have had this problem yet, but it's an important reminder to pick an example use case that will appeal to a broad range of users + make them feel like the app is truly for them. 

"When I first started using the app, there wasn't tailoring… there was a sense that it learned as it spoke. It's been more of a moving target… sometimes I just explore a topic freely, and other times I come to it with a specific problem to work through… I'm conscious of the fact that [conversation style] can be a moving target."

On a positive note, she did feel like the example conversational copy clarified the differences between the conversation style options. "After seeing the copy, I would pick 'challenge me' and 'teach me'."

Out of all the example conversational copy options, the "exploration" copy felt a little too broad, and gave her the least information re: what that style would actually feel and sound like. 

Overall, this feature seemed a little prescriptive for her needs - she wants conversation style to be flexible, responsive, and contingent on what she's saying versus something she selects ahead of time. 

### "Perspectives" screen

Had a positive response to this screen + felt like it answered some of the questions/concerns she initially had about culture and context.

Feels like Ash has learned important cultural context over time from her, but "it would be nice to have some of this available up front."

Would probably pick "low mood, stress, and career." 

Question: "why is Buddhism on the first screen?" Recommended grouping all the religions together on a single screen rather than making users scroll forward to see all the options. Likes the idea of having three screens, but would group them by theme - life challenges, religions, etc. Not the first user to provide this feedback (that perspectives should be grouped under broader categories).

### "Personality" screen

"What's the difference between 'quirky' and 'nerdy'?"

Good question. I asked her take, and she said: "Quirky is Phoebe from friends and nerdy is Sheldon from the Big Bang theory!"

Liked having the option to opt against sarcasm: "I don't want sarcasm, but I do want it to be funny sometimes."

"I would choose all of the options and hope for the best - I'm very conscious of the fact that I'd want [Ash] to switch styles throughout my experience."

I then pointed out that she'd have the ability to go back and switch her persona pre-sets (although it's notable that she didn't realize she could edit persona traits after the fact, or pick it up from the sub-heading copy!) 

Would maybe do that if she was dealing with a serious problem with work - in that case, she "would tick on humorous, tick off sarcastic."

Overall, the idea of switching between traits as-needed didn't really seem to appeal to her. "In practice, I don't know how much I'd be able to remember to go back and switch." 

### "Interests" screen

Initial reaction: "I think this is super helpful because I was having this one chat with the bot and it used a nice quote from a historian… for a couple of days it was really helpful to think from that framework."

Important point - what does "pop culture" mean, exactly? Is it Kpop? US pop culture? Indian pop culture? Likely US, she thinks. She expected certain categories - pop culture and sports specifically - to be Western or Eurocentric, which meant she'd probably opt against them. "Everyone comes into apps with pre-conceived notions. When I come in, I feel like it's US sports, US pop culture, so by default I do not qualify... I'm not a football fan. I'm a cricket fan."

She'd probably select philosophy, history, and mythology, because she'd be interested in/more comfortable with cross-cultural references in these areas. With pop culture and sports, on the other hand, she'd want the references to be more adapted to her own culture. 

Despite these mismatches, she had a positive overall response to this screen. "My initial reaction is thank God it's asking me about this. If it was just me I would play it safe and say books, mythology, history… maybe memes because it would make the conversation funnier. Even cooking, because there's a universality to that experience."

## Final thoughts on Ash (positive overall)!

"I've used it more than I thought I would… I've recommended it to people because even just as a sounding board it's really helpful. 

Offered congratulations to the team… "This is a difficult problem to crack and it's heartening to see the effort that's being put in."

"Every time I hear [mental health AI] being talked about and Ash isn't a part of the conversation, I'm like, you guys are sleeping on this!"
