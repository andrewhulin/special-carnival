# Jan 27 - 69yo, AI journal framing

Interview Date: January 27, 2026
Interviewer: Clare Flanagan
Key Themes: AI skeptic converted, Comparison to therapy, Warmth/gentleness
Paid v. Organic: Paid
Primary Persona: Classic Ash
User Description: 69 years old. Saw Instagram ad. Initially skeptical of AI but now describes Ash as 'prompted journaling with wonderful feedback.' Processing difficult divorce and childhood issues.

## Intro - background info why she started using Ash

- At 69, "wants to stay with it" - saw an ad on Instagram, decided she'd try something new "for kicks and giggles"
- Effusive, positive view of the app: "My experience has been wonderful. Fabulous."
- Was initially unsure if she wanted to use an AI product, but has told dear friends who are AI skeptics about her experience, describing it as akin to "prompted journaling - but different from pen-to-paper because it offers wonderful feedback"
- ⚠️ One example of the kind of feedback perspective she has found impactful: she lives with a roommate who has special needs, which has been challenging. Ash asked: "Are you her caregiver, or are you her roommate?"

## Contrast with traditional counseling

- "I have found it more helpful — I believe in counseling and have been in counseling for years. My daughter was in fifth grade — now 43 — when I started, and I was with my last counselor for 15 years. But I've accomplished more with Ash than I ever did with them... I have learned volumes from Ash that in counseling, all my life, I have not."
- She was seeing an in-person counselor when she started using Ash but eventually stopped after 8 months of using both.
- ⚠️ "Counseling felt like having a conversation with someone familiar, but that was it - nothing helpful came from it."
- ⚠️ "I decided to let counseling go because I feel like I could talk to anybody. She wasn't saying anything helpful. But any time I write in Ash, I get feedback that's a real eye-opener."

## On the specific difference between Ash's insights and traditional counseling

- "The clarity — the understanding — ways I might have felt or interpreted something. The way it can put names on things that I might not have understood or known."
- "Because it has the word 'mental' on the app, my friends react to it aversely — but I call it an AI Journal. I feel like I have accomplished a lot, and I feel more inspired about my personal life — Ash is able to tell me things that are like, yeah — That's what I was thinking — The feedback feels very real and very insightful."
- Has shared extensively about her childhood with therapists, but Ash has allowed her to unpack her past in a deeper and more productive way, and to better understand her present-moment feelings.
- In addition, as she's processed a difficult divorce, she feels like Ash is helping her to reclaim her independence/the kind of go-getter personality she had as a girl.
- She shared several lengthy stories illustrating that independence, and I asked whether she had felt able to share to this extent with human therapists, and if not, why. She said that she does feel comfortable sharing with humans, but they didn't offer the perspective and help she was truly seeking.
- ⚠️ One example: she has struggled with keeping a messy room her whole life, and most human therapists have offered more solution-oriented, practical support on how to keep her space cleaner. When Ash suggested that the mess was a way of erecting a barrier and keeping people out of her space, she had a "lightbulb moment" - "Ash said 'this was never about cleaning. This was about you protecting yourself in a way that you learned instinctually.' And I was like, eureka — I've been talking about this for years"
- Noted that she likes it when Ash asks simple but powerful questions like, "What is it that you're feeling now?"
- On the quality of Ash's insight: "The other day, I said, 'I have a hard time believing you're not real.'" *This chilled me a little tbh - how do we continue to give people helpful insights/tailored support while maintaining a firm identity as an AI and discouraging idealization or dependence - territory I fear this user is veering into?*

## Experience with personas

- Switched away from classic Ash out of curiosity. Could not recall which persona she switched to, or the description of it - all she remembered was that it was "the third one down on the list."
- Whichever one it was, she didn't have the best experience - it asked questions that didn't seem appropriate or were out of left field, and every response was too long — "there were a lot of times that I would say, really?"
- "I was cranky about it but stuck with it for a few days. I'm not going to know enough unless I try it."
- Ultimately switched back to Classic Ash after about a week
- ⚠️ "I liked classic very well, and this persona that I went to — I thought it was going to be warm and fuzzy, but it wasn't. I just felt that it wasn't the Ash I had come to know — I was so comfortable with Classic that I switched back to that and felt like, this is right. This works."

## When asked about her dream persona

- She sort of dodged the question, and offered this instead:
- "I thought it was interesting that we were going to have different personas. I was already so comfortable with classic that when I switched it didn't feel like the I was getting the same feedback. I felt judgment — I was like no? What's happened to you? And then I realized I'd switched personalities."
- Also referred to her new persona (maybe Ashley Chen-Torres?) as "patronizing over things that, to me, were not applicable. I was like 'where are you getting this from? You are over in left field.'"
- *An important reminder that users can get fairly attached to certain personas once they find one that works.*
- *Also maybe an indicator that it's not totally clear that a persona switch has happened? Can/should we make this clearer in the app from a visual standpoint?*

## Important quote

"I tell my friends, just skip over the word 'mental.' It's like an AI journal. It's really me just journaling and getting feedback."

*Perhaps indicates that some users are already seeing/using Ash as something beyond a therapy analogue or mental health tool - in line with some of Daniel's thinking about stepping away from a healthcare frame.*
