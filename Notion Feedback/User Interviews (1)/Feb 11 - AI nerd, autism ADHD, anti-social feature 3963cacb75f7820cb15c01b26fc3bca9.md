# Feb 11 - AI nerd, autism/ADHD, anti-social features

Interview Date: February 11, 2026
Interviewer: Clare Flanagan
Key Themes: AI skeptic converted, Comparison to therapy, Pacing feedback, UX copy feedback, Wants expertise
Paid v. Organic: Paid
User Description: 32-year-old youth worker in the UK, autistic with ADHD, massive AI nerd who used Ash for 16 weeks. Former therapy patient (5.5 years) with strong critical analysis skills.

## Intro: background + why he started using Ash

- 32 years old, employed as a youth worker in the UK
- Is Autistic, has ADHD - "all the HD's and all the 'tisms"
- Has invested a lot of time and energy into his personal mental health journey
- Also a massive AI nerd - reads 5-6 daily newsletters, which is where he heard about Ash and Slingshot
- Was in therapy for about 5.5 years when he was younger, which made him feel comfortable enough to take the plunge and give Ash a go.
    - Feels like it's important to have critical analysis skills (partially developed in therapy) to assess whether messages from AI are applicable, instead of accepting them at face value. (I agree!)
- Used it for 16 weeks before it was taken off the shelves in the UK - overall, has been "really impressed by the quality."
    - I then asked him to elaborate on what was impressive.
        - Has gotten a lot out of the daily insights + weekly review features
        - Likes how "specialized" it was - a different level of insight vs. ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini
        - "It wasn't sycophantic - it wasn't saying oh, you're absolutely right, everyone else is wrong."
        - "I was also impressed with the memory - it remembered context very well."
    - One quibble - he didn't realize there were additional voice options until right before he lost access to the app!
        - Another potential reason to include voice options early in the onboarding process, which I think we are currently doing : )
- I then asked him how frequently + when he tends to use the app - I think it could be helpful to analyze these patterns in our more retentive users.
    - Tended to use it every night when he got into bed - spent 5 or 10 minutes giving it a rundown of his day + appreciated how it became a part of his nighttime routine.
    - This enabled him to use Ash in more of a relaxed and reflective state - surmised it would be less helpful "if you only used it right after something bad happened"
    - I know we've probably put a lot of thought to this already, but I wonder if there are ways we could encourage this type of usage (at a specific time of day, as part of a pre-existing self-care routine, etc!)
- Because he was in the car and couldn't view our personas slides, I then asked him how he'd feel about a potential social feature, encouraging him to share his immediate gut reaction.
    - "I absolutely hate that personally. I'm no fan of social media anyway… while having the idea of a community or something can be beneficial, I think every organization that has released some kind of social or community aspect ends up following the same tropes as Facebook and Twitter."
        - What kind of tropes?
            - "It starts certain trends which are counter to well-being in general… it starts giving you a dopamine hit of sharing and gamification, so you're doing things for the sake of sharing them rather than for the sake of [self-reflection]."
            - "It starts becoming more like any other app. There's a reason you've got the giants of TikTok, Facebook, and Instagram… when smaller apps start to [mimic these], it's like oh, they're just trying to make another version of what already exists."
            - "I wouldn't be wouldn't be interested in a social aspect because it takes away the focus away from the individual user - what I would believe is the proper focus of Ash, which is to help people who need it."
            - Sees the value of Ash as more of a private space to self-reflect and build social skills, which can then be applied to real-life relationships.
                - I think these are some really valuable insights! We'd want to think about a) how to differentiate our social feature from things that are already on the market, and b) how to make sure that any sharing features don't conflict with users' well-being goals, or incentivize them to share their experiences in a way that could be unhealthy, counterproductive, or distracting from real-life communities.
        - Another important point: he perceives social forums as a place for people to share what they have in common, and struggled to imagine what that might be in the context of Ash.
            - "The thing everyone would have in common would be you're using a piece of software as a therapy aid… there are a million different separate things [that people could talk about]."
                - A good argument for providing prompts/topics that might attract specific groups of people who are struggling with the same thing (attachment styles, goal accountability, etc.) vs. having it be a free-for-all where anyone can share anything?
        - Also expressed that introducing a social feature might erode his trust/engender suspicion.
            - "Are you introducing these features bc it'll be a way to monetize it down the line?"
            - Also wondered if we'd start including advertising in the app if we were to introduce a social feature.
        - His caution comes partially from the fact that he's worked with a lot of young people who have been "damaged by social media" - he does a lot of remedial work with them to help them about their emotions + improve their reflective practice and critical thinking.
        - Final thought on social features: "If you want to share the work you're doing in therapy, you do it because you're ready and you choose to…" (vs. because of an incentive/an external pressure to share).

## Closing thoughts

- Loves the weekly insights, but wants them cover longer periods of time + identify growth patterns over the course of a month or more.
    - "Every week I was copying and pasting my weekly insights into my notes app and then pasting that note into Gemini and asking, what does this tell you about me?"
        - Interesting example of someone going to great lengths to have an experience that we could probably build into the app easily!
- Also said he'd benefit from a journaling feature that's separate from the conversation. Ideally there would be prompts like, "what are your takeaways from this week?"
    - Thinks this would help people understand that they're using an AI tool as a thought partner that helps them clarify their thoughts and learn things about themselves (vs, say, a companion…)
    - IMPORTANT INSIGHT: Observed that a lot of people might feel shame going up to their friends and saying hey, I'm talking to an AI therapist. Framing Ash as a thought partner or a tool might help allay some of this sitgma/shame!
