# 68 y/o in RV after house fire - daughter referred her to Ash

Interview Date: February 25, 2026
Interviewer: Andrew Frawley
Key Themes: Been to therapy, Found via friend/family
Paid v. Organic: Organic
Primary Persona: N/A
User Description: Rebecca is a 68-year-old in Texas living in an RV after her house burned down. She has PTSD, ADHD, and chronic pain. Her daughter recommended Ash after finding it more helpful than two years of therapy. Rebecca used it once for 3 hours and is already planning to use it jointly with her daughter.

### Intro & Background

Rebecca lives on her property in Texas in an RV while a new double-wide is being placed — her house burned down on her birthday in July. She's about to start living with her 31-year-old daughter, her daughter's fiancé (with whom she has a volatile history), and a 7-month-old grandbaby. She has significant apprehension about the move-in given a prior blowup involving the fiancé getting in her face, which triggered her PTSD.

Her daughter Savannah has been using Ash and told Rebecca: *"Mom, I've been using this app that I think has helped me more than the two years of therapy that I've been through."* Rebecca tried it — had one roughly three-hour session — and found immediate value, particularly around reframing her fear of upcoming pain management injections.

She's 68 (born 1956 based on email), deals with PTSD, ADHD, and chronic back pain. She's on a fixed/limited income (she pays the mortgage, works intermittently) and is navigating complex family dynamics largely on her own.

---

### Background with Mental Health / Self-Development

Rebecca has tried therapy multiple times with mostly poor results. Her experiences mirror several other interviewees — passive therapists who offered no real feedback:

> *"The last guy I saw, he would just sit there on the computer and I couldn't see what he was doing. He could have been playing a game or stockbroker, I don't know… I would talk for about fifty-five minutes about just random shit. And he'd go, okay, well, let's book our next appointment. So I just quit going."*
> 

She also noted the reflexive push toward medication: *"The first thing they wanna do is send you to the psychiatrist to get medication. I didn't wanna take it."* Her one positive therapy experience was with a social worker who had practical resources to offer — suggesting she values actionable help over open-ended talk.

She has ADHD, which she self-identifies as affecting her ability to stay on topic in sessions: *"My brain just kinda bounces around. I can keep a conversation going and never really complete a thought."* This is relevant to why Ash's text-based, asynchronous format may work better for her than live talk therapy.

No mention of self-development books or prior AI use for mental health.

### Marketing & Expectations

**Discovery:** Direct word-of-mouth from her daughter, who is also an active Ash user.

**Expectations going in:** She was apprehensive about AI generally — *"I'm kind of apprehensive about AI and how much information it could get from me and use against me. I have nightmares about robots."* — but trusted her daughter's endorsement enough to try it.

**What Ash does right:**

- **Concise, actionable reframing.** Her pain management example was striking — Ash helped her shift from dreading the shots to looking forward to them by reframing toward the outcome: *"Instead of focusing on that aspect of the procedure, to try to get myself through the pain and to the other side where I actually don't have the pain in my back anymore. So I've been actually looking forward to the shots."*
- **Pinpoints solutions vs. open-ended exploration.** She explicitly values Ash's directness over therapy's tendency to drag: *"If I come to him… he will be more able to pinpoint solutions than to have me try to think, well, what is my best idea about the situation? Because my best ideas about things have gotten me into a lot of trouble."*
- **Efficiency.** *"Therapy drags on way too long on certain things."* Ash cuts to the point.

**What Ash gets wrong / could improve:**

- **Too narrow in scope.** Her clearest product wish: *"I would like you to be more broad spectrum. If I have a question about something I would ask another AI app for — like Grok or ChatGPT — if I could just ask it random questions that don't have anything to do with mental health."* She wants Ash to be a general-purpose companion, not siloed.
- **Data/privacy anxiety.** She asked directly whether a human was at the keyboard and expressed real concern about data use. She didn't recall the privacy opt-in during onboarding, suggesting the flow may not be salient enough for less tech-savvy users.

---

### Product Positioning

**Companion vs. clinical:** Rebecca leans toward broad companion. She wants Ash to expand beyond mental health into a general assistant she can rely on — essentially replacing her need to use multiple AI apps.

**Unique use case — joint family tool:** Rebecca's most distinctive idea is using Ash as a **mediation layer** between herself and her daughter. She envisions them each talking to Ash separately about shared conflicts, then comparing insights together: *"Maybe Ash might be the perfect solution for a conjunction point for my daughter and I to work through issues before they become problematic."* She's strategic about it too — she wants it to be her daughter's idea so it doesn't get dismissed.

**Social/community feature:** Not asked directly in this interview.

**Pricing sensitivity:** Not raised explicitly, but her financial situation (fixed income, living in an RV, paying the mortgage alone) suggests price sensitivity. She's a single-session user so far — retention will depend on continued free access or very low cost.

### Closing Insights & Strategic Takeaways

**For marketing & positioning:**
Rebecca is a **referral-driven, late-adopter user** who would never have found Ash on her own. She's AI-skeptical, not on social media, and distrustful of technology generally. But her daughter's personal endorsement — framed as "this helped me more than two years of therapy" — was powerful enough to overcome all of that. This is the strongest word-of-mouth acquisition story in these interviews and suggests that **user-to-family-member referral** is a high-potential growth channel, especially for older demographics.

**On the "daily tool" question:**
Rebecca has only used Ash once, so daily usage isn't established. But her vision for it is already expansive — she wants it as a general companion, a pain management support, and a family mediation tool. If Ash broadens its scope even slightly (or just loosens the guardrails on non-mental-health topics), users like Rebecca could become multi-use-case daily users organically.

**Key product signals:**

- **The "joint use" concept** — two family members using Ash separately to navigate shared conflict — is a novel use case that could be a feature or at least a marketed benefit. It sidesteps the awkwardness of couples/family therapy while leveraging Ash's memory and consistency.
- **Onboarding privacy flow** needs to be more memorable/explicit for users who are anxious about AI and data. Rebecca didn't recall it, which means it didn't reassure her.
- **ADHD + text format** is an underexplored fit. Rebecca self-identifies as someone whose brain bounces around in live conversation — Ash's asynchronous, text-based format may be structurally better for neurodiverse users than traditional talk therapy.

**Acquisition insight:**
First pure word-of-mouth referral in these interviews. Daughter-to-mother pipeline. The referral message — "this helped me more than two years of therapy" — is essentially a ready-made ad.
